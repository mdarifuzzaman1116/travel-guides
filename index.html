🇺🇸 U.S. State Dept Travel Advisory
⚠️ Level 2 — Exercise Increased Caution

Crime is the primary concern, not terrorism. Same advisory level as Costa Rica, Paris, and London — it's location-specific risk, not country-wide.

  • Avoid: South side of Belize City (south of Haulover Creek) — highest violent crime concentration
  • Safe tourist zones: Ambergris Caye, Caye Caulker, Placencia, Hopkins, San Ignacio
  • Crime types: Armed robbery, sexual assault, home invasion — mostly gang-related
  • Local police: Under-resourced; most crimes go unresolved
  • Emergency medical: Severely limited — serious illness requires medevac to another country
  • Advisory updated: March 12, 2026 — level unchanged
Full Advisory Register STEP
🏥 CDC Health Advisory
  • Malaria: Risk in rural/jungle areas — consult doctor for prophylaxis before travel
  • Dengue: Present year-round, higher risk in rainy season — use DEET religiously
  • Zika: Active in Belize — critical for anyone planning future pregnancies
  • Hepatitis A: Vaccine recommended for all travelers
  • Typhoid: Recommended, especially outside major resorts
  • Measles (MMR): Must be up to date — global measles advisory active May 2025
  • New World Screwworm: Reported in Belize — avoid contact with livestock wounds
  • Water: Drink bottled water only — tap unsafe for visitors
  • Infant note: At 13 months, baby needs age-appropriate mosquito protection, pediatrician consult before travel
CDC Belize Page
🛂 Entry Requirements
  • Visa: No visa required for U.S. citizens — up to 30 days free, extendable
  • Passport: Valid for 6 months beyond departure date
  • Proof of return: Onward/return ticket required at check-in
  • Infant: Birth certificate required; if only one parent traveling, bring notarized letter of consent from non-traveling parent
  • Departure tax: ~$40 USD per person included in most airline tickets — verify at booking
  • Medications: Bring prescription documentation; bring ALL medications from home — shortages common in Belize
  • No HIV/AIDS entry restrictions for U.S. visitors

💡 Insider Tips
🦟
Mosquito Protection is Non-Negotiable
DEET 30%+ for adults. Use Picaridin-based repellent on baby clothing and gear — NOT directly on baby's skin. Dengue risk is real in Sep.
🌀
Monitor NOAA Daily
Download the NOAA app and Windy before departure. Check forecasts every morning in September. Have an inland retreat plan ready (San Ignacio is safe).
📱
Get a Local SIM Card
Belize Telemedia (BTL) sells affordable SIM cards at the airport. $20 USD gets you data for a week. Cell coverage is decent on the cayes and towns.
💵
USD Accepted Everywhere
BZD is fixed at exactly 2:1 USD — you'll always get BZ$2 per US$1. USD is accepted almost everywhere. Change is often given in BZD.
🐊
Respect Wildlife Warnings
Belize has crocodiles in lagoons and rivers — do NOT swim in non-designated areas. Sep rains push crocs toward populated areas.
⛵
Water Taxis Can Cancel
In September storms, water taxi service between mainland and cayes suspends. Always have a mainland backup plan and never travel to an island during storm warning.
🏥
Medical Evacuation Insurance is Essential
Belize has no advanced trauma care. Serious illness requires medevac to Houston or Miami. Get MedJetAssist or similar evacuation coverage.
🎪
Carnival is September 10 Week
The annual Carnival and St. George's Caye Day (Sep 10) national holiday make September culturally vibrant. Belize City and Belmopan host the biggest celebrations.
🦞
Lobster Season Active
Lobster season in Belize runs June to February. September is prime time — order lobster everywhere, it's abundant, fresh, and reasonably priced.
☔
Rain Is Morning + Overnight
Typical September pattern: rain in early morning and overnight; afternoons often clear. Schedule outdoor activities for 10 AM–3 PM for best weather windows.
🌿
Inland Is Safer from Storms
San Ignacio (Cayo District) is the go-to inland retreat. Higher elevation, Maya ruins, jungle lodges, and river activities — plus hurricane-safe location.
💳
Cash-First Destination
Many smaller restaurants, tour operators, and markets are cash-only. ATMs exist in San Pedro and Belize City but can be unreliable. Bring $400–$600 USD cash.
👶 Infant Travel Notes

Your daughter will be approximately 13 months old in September 2026

  • Lap infant status: Under 24 months — no separate seat needed on flights (~10% adult fare each way)
  • Documents needed: Birth certificate required at Belize immigration; if only one parent travels, bring notarized consent letter
  • Bug protection: Use Picaridin on her clothing only — DEET is not safe for infants under 2. Keep her fully covered during peak mosquito hours (dawn/dusk).
  • Hydration + heat: September is hot and humid. Carry water constantly; watch for heat signs in infant (excessive fussiness, no wet diapers).
  • Malaria precaution: Consult her pediatrician before travel — infant malaria protection options are very limited; avoiding jungle interiors at dawn/dusk is safest.
  • Medical care: Bring a full pediatric first aid kit, infant fever/pain medication, oral rehydration salts. Advanced infant care is unavailable in Belize.
  • Baby gear: Pack a baby carrier/soft carrier (better than a stroller on uneven cayes and jungle paths). Most resorts provide cribs on request.
  • Hurricane plan: In September, always have a solid evacuation plan. Never be stranded on a caye with an infant during a storm watch — move inland immediately.
  • Baby-friendly areas: Ambergris Caye (golf carts, flat ground), Placencia (calm lagoon side), Chaa Creek eco-resort. Avoid remote jungle lodges without solid access.
